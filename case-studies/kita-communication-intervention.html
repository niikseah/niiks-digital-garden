Communications for social change · Kita

From blind box to brochure

Twelve-week team project for Kita (Singapore non-profit for at-risk youth): a portable advocacy toolkit—a blind box with mascot keychain that unfolds into a brochure—built to start real conversations, fit tight non-profit economics, and respect youth agency (no pity-led “sob story” framing).

Client
Kita (formerly Hatch)
Team
5 · research, concept, visual design, prototype, 3D demo
Timeline
12 weeks, end-to-end

01 Situation

A cause people would back — if only they knew it existed.

Kita’s work is strong, but formal advocacy infrastructure is thin: growth depends on advocates and donors who mostly discover the org through word of mouth. Existing brochures read like “terminal documents”—they close conversations instead of opening them.

Stakeholder interview (board member Jim See): advocates want to talk about Kita, but lack something tangible to hand over. The brief became a conversation starter, not a one-way info dump—while avoiding the sector default of pity narratives that clash with Kita’s emphasis on agency and co-creation.

02 Task

Turn sympathisers into ambassadors.

Design an artefact an advocate would carry, give away, and use as an opening line—usable across donor profiles (digital micro-givers to institutional partners). Scope: research, concept, visual design, physical prototype, interactive 3D demo, and a mixed-methods evaluation plan.

Constraints: non-profit unit economics, small-batch production, durability “handbag survival,” and configurability so youth beneficiaries can personalise pieces—co-authors of the story, not its subject.

03 Research & framing

Evidence, segments, and language that shaped the artefact.

Desk research + competitive SWOT (e.g. Praxium, Trybe) showed a gap: peers rely on text-heavy one-pagers; almost nobody optimises for shareability or object memorability. GiveAsia / NVPC data informed donor segmentation; one collateral had to flex across very different motivations.

Jim See’s interview surfaced a design rule: advocates calibrate messaging live—so the toolkit needed modular hooks, not a single fixed script. A language audit (with Kita’s team) flagged terms like “empower” as misaligned; copy favoured language that foregrounds space, co-creation, and potential.

04 Research findings

Five synthesis points with “what it means” implications.

Visibility beyond existing networks; tailoring to diverse donor and advocate motivations; foregrounding youth agency and co-creation; language that avoids deficit framing; and one grounded document that consolidates pathways to help—each steers the blind box + brochure format, copy, and modularity.

05 Key design decisions

Every choice tested against a stated constraint.

Format

Blind box, not a flat brochure

Unboxing creates emotional memory; the object keeps living on a desk as a passive touchpoint. Trade-off: higher per-unit cost vs longer engagement lifecycle.

Information architecture

Twelve surfaces, one brochure

Unfolding yields 12 panels. Proximity + visual hierarchy segment mission, impact, pathways to help, and CTAs—skimmable depth for mid-conversation wayfinding.

Identity

Mascot keychains (Bubu, Roro, Kiki, Pebbie)

Inspired by leadership wearing character pins at events: pick a character, pair with an origin card—small act of self-expression that starts chats.

Configurability

Designed to be co-authored

Deliberate empty fields for youth to complete (infrastructuring / participatory design)—so the toolkit doesn’t speak for beneficiaries in a way that undercuts the thesis.

Assembly stays straightforward for ops; thicker stock for durability; companion 3D demo shows how the fold works in the wild.

The toolkit should convert interest into action — a base document an advocate can carry, not a statement they have to memorise.

06 Impact & evaluation

How we’d know if it works.

Planned outcomes: stronger advocate knowledge/confidence when representing Kita; measurable lift in traffic to site and donation channels.

Methods (planned): pre/post surveys for existing advocates; surveys for new advocates after toolkit receipt; platform analytics before/after distribution; light field observation at outreach; follow-up focus groups; longitudinal check-in (6–12 months) for sustained use.

07 What we’d do differently

Honest next steps.

  • More advocate interviews sooner — one deep conversation with Jim See carried a lot of weight; a wider spread (esp. younger advocates) would stress-test assumptions earlier.
  • User testing with youths — validate configurability and tone with the people the toolkit ultimately advocates for.

Each blind box contains a character keychain, based on Kita's mascots. Unfolded, the blind box transforms into a brochure informing readers more on Kita as an organisation.

01 Situation

A cause people would back — if only they knew it existed.

Kita does transformative work with at-risk youth in Singapore, but relies almost entirely on word-of-mouth advocacy. Kita confronts a critical strategic challenge that threatens its long-term sustainability: a significant deficit in its formal communication and advocacy infrastructure.

Target audience

To craft a scalable solution that fits into Kita's compelling narrative, potential advocates and donors are targeted; Empowered by the financial capital and influential networks they yield, these individuals have the ability to amplify Kita's reach.

💡

This reflects a core tenet of stakeholder theory, which posits that an organisation's long-term viability is dependent on its ability to effectively manage relationships with all groups that supply critical resources, not just those who are the primary beneficiaries of its services (Freeman, 1984).

Our interview with board member and advocate Jim See surfaced the central tension: advocates want to talk about Kita, but the existing materials don't give them anything to hand over. Traditional brochures read like terminal documents — one-way, informational, quietly forgettable. They close conversations rather than open them.

Traditional brochures act as one-way, terminal points of information. They operate on an outdated diffusion model of communication insufficient for motivating organic, peer-to-peer advocacy Kita requires. Recognising that Kita's fundamental issue was not a lack of information, but a lack of means for conversation starting and sharing, we decided that a shift towards a more participatory communication strategy was needed to change these passive sympathisers into active ambassadors (Servaes, 1996).

The primary objective is to create a resource that acts not as a final statement, but as a conversation starter. This toolkit must be engaging, aesthetically compelling, and easily shareable, motivating potential advocates not only to learn about Kita's mission but to feel personally inspired to champion it within their own professional and social circles.

Compounding the problem, the non-profit sector's default rhetorical tool — the sob story — was explicitly off-limits. Kita's ethos centres on agency and co-creation; framing youth as victims would undermine the product of its own work. We had to design for persuasion without pity.

02 Task

Design an artefact that turns sympathisers into ambassadors.

Not a brochure. A tool an advocate would carry in their bag, hand to a stranger, and use as the opening line of a conversation — across donor profiles that range from digitally-native micro-givers to senior institutional partners.

Scope: research, concept, visual design, physical prototype, 3D interactive demo, and a mixed-methods evaluation plan — delivered in a twelve-week academic semester to a live non-profit client.

Constraints: tight unit economics (non-profit budget), production-ready at small batch sizes, durable enough to survive a handbag, and configurable so Kita's youth beneficiaries could personalise the toolkit themselves — making them co-authors of the advocacy rather than its subject.

03 Research & framing

Desk research

Academic and non-academic sources within the 3 main themes of Kita's organisational context, Singapore's at-risk youth, and Singapore advocate and donor profiles.

  1. To analyse Kita's organisational context, we reviewed the official websites, online publications, and social media platforms of Kita, its predecessor Hatch, and those of comparable organisations. Using a purposive sampling approach, we selected Praxium and Trybe due to their close alignment with Kita's work. A SWOT analysis of each organisation's branding materials, presentation of programmes and outreach methods was then conducted.
  2. To analyse Singapore advocate and donor profiles, we reviewed publicly available data on online donation platforms such as GiveAsia and relevant online reports on philanthropic trends. We focused on both younger, digitally-savvy donors and older, more established contributors, as well as volunteers and partners with varying levels of engagement.

Competitive SWOT analysis across Praxium and Trybe — both adjacent youth-agency organisations — surfaced a consistent blind spot: most non-profits in the category lean on text-heavy one-pagers. Nobody in the adjacent set was designing for shareability or object memorability. That gap became our position.

Stakeholder interview

An interview was conducted with one of Kita's board members, Kwek Jim See, to gather first-hand insights from an existing advocate. Questions were prepared to understand the demographics, behaviour and preferences of advocates, as well as to gain insight into the current information flows between advocates and potential advocates.

A deep interview with Jim See, an existing Kita advocate and board member, gave us the single most useful insight in the project: advocates calibrate messaging to their audience in real time. A successful toolkit couldn't prescribe one narrative — it had to give advocates portable, modular hooks they could pick up and drop based on the person in front of them.

Donor segmentation

Public data from GiveAsia and NVPC's National Giving Study sliced the donor base: younger digitally-savvy micro-donors, older consistent givers, youth volunteers seeking bite-sized involvement, and institutional partners needing measurable impact. A single piece of collateral had to work across all four — which is why a multi-component toolkit (object, brochure, card) beat a single monolithic document.

Language audit

Kita's team cautioned us against "empower" — the word implies powerlessness. We built a small internal glossary of preferred verbs (foreground, foster, make space for, co-create) that shaped every line of copy on the final artefact. A small thing that mattered disproportionately.

04 Research findings

What desk research and interviews converged on.

Five synthesis points from Kita’s context, donor and advocate data, stakeholder interviews, and language norms—each with implications for the toolkit’s role, tone, and structure.

Finding 1

As a recently established non-profit organisation, with minimal press and social media presence, awareness of Kita remains limited beyond its existing support networks.

What it means for us

This visibility gap suggests the need for a toolkit that acts as a clear and effective entry point; The toolkit needed to be eye-catching and informative enough to spark and sustain conversations.

Finding 2

Advocate profiles and preferences shape engagement strategies. Donors include younger, digitally-savvy individuals who prefer online micro-donations and older donors who provide larger, consistent contributions (Kita's GiveAsia, 2024). Interviews with Kita's team and advocate, Jim See, highlighted the need to tailor messaging based on differing motivations and levels of familiarity with the social service landscape.

What it means for us

The toolkit needed to cater to these diverse needs with visually engaging, interactive resources that outline different avenues for support.

Finding 3

Kita's youth-led agency approach emerged as a strong point of differentiation. Research on at-risk youth revealed that their experiences stemmed from a combination of both social and familial factors including intergenerational offending and educational pressures. This highlights the need for approaches that give youths genuine involvement and a voice in shaping their own pathways. Kita's unique participatory approach grants youths agency in the creation of spaces and community, which was emphasised by Jim See who used key words like "agency" and "co-creation" to pique the interest of others when sharing about Kita.

What it means for us

This highlighted the importance of foregrounding Kita's collaborative ethos in the toolkit.

Finding 4

The choice of language used when referring to at-risk youth is crucial. Framing narratives around strength and potential, rather than deficits or "sob stories," helps advocates communicate Kita's mission authentically without tapping on pity or sympathy. The interview with Kita's team cautioned to avoid terms like "empower" in the communication intervention as it implied that the youths were "powerless" before. Instead, Kita prefers language that spotlights how the organisation provides space and opportunities for youths to reach their full potential.

Finding 5

The research revealed a lack of accessible pathways and readily consolidated information for potential advocates to learn or engage with Kita. Both Kita's team and Jim See noted the need for a single, grounded document that brings together key information about Kita and clear ways for advocates to contribute.

05 Key design decisions

Every choice was made against a stated constraint.

Decision 01 · Format

A blind box, not a brochure.

Why: the sector's default — a folded leaflet — loses the reader at the moment of handover. A blind box forces interaction: the unboxing moment creates emotional memory, and the object lives on a desk afterwards as a passive touchpoint. Trade-off: higher per-unit cost, offset by a longer engagement lifecycle per unit distributed.

Decision 02 · Information architecture

Twelve surfaces, one brochure.

Why: unfolding the box yields twelve discrete panels. We used proximity and visual hierarchy to segment content — mission, impact, pathways to help, contact — so an advocate could flip to the relevant panel mid-conversation without re-reading the whole thing. Skimmable depth, not dense depth.

Decision 03 · Identity system

Character keychains as a pinboard for identity.

Why: inspired by Kita's executive director wearing character pins to conferences as conversation openers. Four mascots (Bubu, Roro, Kiki, Pebbie) with accompanying origin cards let advocates pick the character that resonates with them — turning the toolkit into a small act of self-expression.

Decision 04 · Configurability

Designed to be co-authored, not finished.

Why: Kita's mission is youth agency. A toolkit that spoke for them would have undermined the thesis. Drawing from Dantec & DiSalvo's infrastructuring framework, we left deliberate empty fields the youths could fill in — making them the final designers of their own advocacy materials.

Communication Intervention

Our advocacy toolkit includes a brochure in the form of a blind box with the theme of home, as well as an identity artefact in the form of a character keychain and a matching card. When unfolded, the blind box transforms into a brochure that can be read to gain a deeper understanding of Kita.

The keychain is modelled after Kita's four characters (Bubu, Roro, Kiki, Pebbie). Accompanying each keychain is a matching card detailing the character's origins and what it represents about Kita. We were inspired by Victor Zhu, Kita's executive director, and how he wears Kita's character-themed pins to conferences to start conversations and leave lasting impressions. Keychains were chosen as merchandise due to their conspicuous and attractive appearance, as well as their flexibility in display.

The blind box incorporates Kita's distinct visual identity and a unique medium of a blind box to stand out amongst other advocacy materials, reinforce brand recognition and recall. Having an interactive unboxing experience extends engagement beyond passive information consumption, creating emotional resonance that promotes information retention and keeps users interested in understanding Kita's mission.

Design Process

Design process

Comprehensiveness — skimmable depth across twelve facets

Focus: information on the brochure needs to be easily digestible and effective in conveying Kita's message. The blind box's 12 sides can clearly segregate categories of information by proximity, and incorporate a visual hierarchy to guide readers' eyes towards critical information and clear calls to action. Copy: depth and skimmability were balanced to give readers a clear overview of Kita. Research had also flagged accessibility and visibility gaps in how people first encounter advocacy materials; an interactive 3D prototype demonstrates how the box unfolds so the fold logic is legible before anyone opens a physical unit.

Design process

Durability — portable, compact, and durable

Focus: advocates bring the toolkit around to facilitate conversations around Kita when opportunities arise, so it needs to stay compact and lightweight. To be carried repeatedly, it also has to be durable—housing the artifacts safely. That durability lets it double as a display item, extending its lifecycle and creating touchpoints for further sharing of Kita when the blind box lives in advocates' spaces. Assembly & ops: we kept the physical design straightforward to assemble and inventory at small batch scale.

Design process

Co-creation with Kita's youths

Focus: most importantly, since Kita's core mission centres on co-creation and youth agency. Without any opportunity to collaborate with Kita's youth beneficiaries, we wanted our design to be configurable to incorporate the concept of "infrastructuring" (Dantec & DiSalvo, 2013). With flexibility built into the design, youths can personalise and adapt it to reflect their own interpretations of what Kita means to them. This intentionality builds a more genuine toolkit meaningful to Kita's youths, sending a unified message to potential supporters of Kita. Rather than prescribing a fixed solution, our intervention provides a foundation and template with elements deliberately left empty so youths can customise it. This explicitly embodies Kita's core values beyond a mere symbolic gesture by creating genuine opportunities for youths to claim ownership over Kita's advocacy narrative, transforming them from passive beneficiaries into active co-creators of how Kita is represented to external stakeholders.

Our advocacy toolkit strikes a delicate balance between innovation and practical organisational needs, and youthfulness with professionalism to avoid undermining the seriousness of Kita's cause. The blind box concept is unconventional but culturally relevant, and its innovativeness and trendiness seek to drive social sharing in today's digital age. The blind box presents a memorable first impression through a palatable entry point into Kita, transforming a generic brochure into a memorable touchpoint.

06 Impact & evaluation
Improve our current advocates' knowledge of Kita and instil confidence in them to communicate Kita's mission to other potential advocates within their network.
Promote measurable engagement outcomes such as increased traffic to Kita's site and donation platforms.

Ultimately, the toolkits aims to be both an information source and a catalyst for potential advocates to invest their interest and for current advocates to sustain their interest in Kita. As such, we plan to utilise a mixed-method approach to evaluate the effectiveness of our toolkit and whether the outcomes are achieved.

We will conduct surveys for both our current advocates and for new advocates supporting Kita after receiving the toolkits. For current advocates, a pre and post-survey will be conducted, once before receiving the toolkit as a knowledge and confidence check in their role as an advocate, and once after receiving the toolkit to measure whether there is an improvement in the advocate's knowledge and confidence in their role. For new advocates, the survey would serve to understand their knowledge and confidence as an advocate for Kita. The types of questions in this survey would revolve around perceived knowledge of Kita, frequency of advocacy related activities, frequency of donation, and motivations behind becoming an advocate and what sustains their interest. Additionally, we can track engagement metrics with Kita's platforms such as their social media, website, and donation channels before and after the toolkit distribution to measure any numerical impact from the toolkits. As such, we will be able to evaluate shifts in the advocates' knowledge and confidence levels in representing Kita and its associated values through an analysis of the survey results and engagement metrics.
Simple field observations can be conducted during Kita outreach events attended by advocates where observations of how advocates interact and utilise the toolkit can inform the effectiveness of the toolkit in practice. Additionally, focus group interviews with advocates can be executed after a certain period of toolkit distribution to evaluate how the advocates utilise the toolkit in their own capacities. These interviews would help us identify which elements in the toolkit help the advocates carry out their role better and any pain points that can be improved upon for subsequent engagements. Tying in both methods would be a longitudinal evaluation of the toolkit's effectiveness. We can approach the advocates after six months to a year to assess the toolkit's impact and whether it is sustained beyond its initial distribution.

With a mixed-method approach for evaluation, we are hence able to gain a well-rounded understanding of the operational and relational success of the toolkit in serving its purpose in achieving its outcomes of improving our current advocates' knowledge and confidence in communicating about Kita and promoting measurable engagement outcomes such as increased traffic to Kita's site and donation platforms.

07 What We'd do differently

Moving forward...

This project demonstrates the benefits of co-creation in projects, especially those related to social good. Direct engagement with Kita's advocates allowed us to gain a clearer understanding of the motivations and preferences that enable effective advocacy. Active collaboration further strengthened the alignment between organisational needs and design outcomes, ensuring the toolkit communicated both mission clairity and accessible pathways for involvement.

i

More interviews would grant us a deeper understanding of Kita's advocates

Jim See's insights were excellent but also load-bearing. A second and third interview — particularly with a younger, less established advocate — would have stress-tested assumptions we ended up designing around. If I had the timeline again, I'd front-load three to five short conversations instead of one deep one.

ii

User testing with youths would enhance the brochure's robustness

Ultimately, we are advocating for the youths. If given more time, we would wish to develop the configurability principle of the blind box. The proposed "co-creation" aspect aligns with both our project's working style and Kita's values.
