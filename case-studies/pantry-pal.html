Users won't download a pantry app. They will download a calorie counter — and log their groceries as a side-effect.

Existing apps (Treatsure, Olio) fix waste after purchase. KitchenPal fixes it before — but requires users to already care. Pantry Pal pairs a pantry manager with a diet tracker, so the acquisition hook is a habit users already have, and the waste-reduction behaviors ride along for free.

Chapter 01 · Context

A waste problem the city already knew about

Singapore's Semakau landfill is projected to run out of space by 2035. The government has a Zero Waste Masterplan, the NEA runs annual reduction campaigns, and NGOs like ZeroWasteSG have been educating consumers for years. And yet food waste in 2023 — 755,000 tonnes — was higher than in 2020.

The education-first approach had hit a ceiling. My interview subjects, both 23-year-old Singaporeans, confirmed it in the bluntest possible way: they knew exactly why food waste mattered, and they still didn't change their behavior. The gap between knowledge and action was the real problem — and no existing app was addressing it for the demographic that needed it most.

Target audience

Pantry Pal is designed for youths aged 18-24 navigating independent food decisions for the first time. This group is highly mobile-first and goal-oriented, but often lacks lightweight systems for pantry visibility, expiry tracking, and meal planning.

  • Who: University students and young working adults.
  • Needs: Fast grocery input, clear pantry awareness, and practical meal planning support.
  • Constraints: Time pressure, inconsistent routines, and low tolerance for complex setup.
  • Desired outcome: Reduce food waste without adding friction to everyday life.

I knew about it, I just didn't care enough to do anything differently.

That quote — paraphrased from interview #1 — reframed the brief. The question wasn't "how do we teach people about food waste?" It was "how do we get people who already know to act?"

Chapter 02 · The strategy

A Trojan horse for behavior change

The competitive landscape had a gap and a cautionary tale, both relevant.

The gap: Treatsure and Olio addressed waste after purchase — connecting consumers to surplus food that already existed. But most household waste is generated upstream of that, when people buy more than they need in the first place.

The cautionary tale: KitchenPal was the only pre-purchase pantry manager in the market. It hadn't scaled. The reason, I suspected, was that pantry management is a chore nobody opens an app for. If the motivation gap was the real problem, building a better pantry app was solving the wrong thing.

So I stopped designing a pantry app.

I designed a diet tracker that happens to have a pantry in it. Diet tracking — by way of apps like MyFitnessPal — is already a daily habit for a significant slice of 18–24-year-olds. Logging a meal means logging ingredients. Logging ingredients means knowing what's in your pantry. Knowing what's in your pantry means not over-buying. The waste-reduction behaviors ride the rails of a motivation that already exists.

This is the single most important decision in the project. Everything else follows from it.

Pantry Pal is a dynamic mobile app designed to simplify pantry management and meal planning while reducing food waste at the point of purchase. By integrating essential features for efficient meal prep and inventory tracking, the app empowers users to make smarter, more sustainable purchasing decisions. Pantry Pal goes beyond basic functionality, integrating complementary features that make it easier to adopt waste-reducing habits, encouraging consistent use, and fostering long-term behavioural change. This holistic approach not only supports sustainability but also fits seamlessly into users' daily lives, making Pantry Pal more effective and practical.

Chapter 03 · Design Process

Design Process

Barriers to Action: Understanding Youths' Disconnect with Food Waste

2 semi-structured interviews were conducted with members of the target audience to gain deeper insights into the attitudes of Singaporean youths toward food waste and to uncover the underlying reasons behind their lack of engagement in waste-reducing practices.

Based on the interviews, several key themes emerged:

  1. Awareness of Environmental Impact of Food Waste
  2. Lack of Immediate Consequences
  3. Convenience over Sustainability
  4. Tediousness of Waste-Reducing Practices

An empathy map and a persona were created using the interview findings to better understand the feelings and perspectives of my interviewees.

Existing User Flow

John Tan heads to the grocery store to buy groceries for the week. He browses through the aisles and picks up food as he goes, impulsively purchasing items based on his preferences and promotions, such as a frozen pizza offer. After shopping, he heads home and begins unpacking his groceries, only to realize that his parents had already bought the frozen pizza earlier, and he didn't check with them beforehand. Lack of family coordination leads to unnecessary duplicate purchases. Additionally, John forgets to buy some key ingredients he had intended to use for the week, which leads to another trip to the store. Forgetfulness in planning causes additional time and effort. A few weeks later, he notices that he has over-purchased and some items have expired because he didn't track their expiry dates. Over-purchasing and expiry management issues lead to food waste.

Proposed User Flow

John Tan starts by planning his meals for the week within the app. Meal planning helps him stay organized and focused. The app automatically generates a grocery list based on the meals he selects and the ingredients he already has at home. Smart shopping list generation ensures he only buys what is needed. He doesn't need to ask his family about their grocery needs, as their purchases are already integrated into the shared list. Family coordination is streamlined, avoiding duplicate purchases. During his shopping trip, John scans, types, or uses voice input to add items into the app's virtual shopping cart. Real-time shopping cart tracking ensures he stays on top of what has been added. As he picks up items, the app updates his grocery list in real time, ensuring that he knows exactly which items are still needed. Real-time updates make the shopping process more efficient.

When John sees the frozen pizza on promotion, the app alerts him that it's already on the list - thanks to the shared grocery feature with his parents. Over-purchasing prevention reduces unnecessary purchases. After checking out, the app helps John store his groceries properly by automatically categorizing them into the correct pantry sections - refrigerated, frozen, or pantry. Food storage guidance ensures that items last longer, reducing food waste.

Once at home, John can use the app to track his inventory: where each item is stored, how much of each item he has, and the expiration dates of everything in his pantry. Inventory and expiry management allows him to stay on top of his food supplies and avoid spoilage. The app also tracks his calories based on the meals he prepares, helping him stay on track with his bulking goals. Calorie and meal tracking offer an integrated solution for his dietary needs.

Prototypes

Key Features

  1. Diet Tracker: The app integrates meal tracking with pantry management, allowing users to track calories while ensuring groceries are purchased and consumed efficiently.
  2. Meal Planner: Users can plan meals, generate accurate shopping lists, and avoid impulse buying, reducing food waste by buying only what's needed.
  3. Expiry Date Tracker: Timely expiry date reminders and recipe suggestions based on expiring ingredients help users minimize waste and use food before it spoils.
  4. Family Integration: The app enables family members to share and update the pantry and grocery list, preventing duplicate purchases and improving coordination.
  5. Ease of Input: Multiple input options, including barcode scanning, voice input, and a comprehensive grocery database, make managing the pantry quick and hassle-free.
  6. Proper Storage Methods: The app categorizes groceries based on storage needs, ensuring proper food organization and reducing spoilage.

Lo-Fi Prototype

User Testing Results

  1. Onboarding - User Details: Participants found the onboarding process clear and intuitive. The Diet Tracker feature was easily understood, with one participant drawing a comparison to MyFitnessPal, which validated the feature's approach.
  2. Onboarding - Family Details: The concept of Family was understood without the need for additional tooltips. While this was clear, I decided to keep the information visible for greater clarity and ease of understanding.
  3. Pantry Navigation: The navigation bar was generally useful, but one participant suggested placing the Pantry button in the center for easier access. I implemented this change while keeping the Profile button on the right as the least accessed.
  4. Adding Groceries to Pantry/Cart: One participant suggested that the grocery list should reflect past inputs for convenience. Users also struggled with a Cancel button that looked like clear, so it was redesigned as Back. Multiple input methods (voice/barcode/text) were positively received for one-handed shopping contexts.
  5. Expired Groceries: A/B testing favored grid view for identifying items by packaging. Users preferred a smaller bottom-left XX Days Left indicator. This also led to an Ingredients page to help find recipes based on expiring items.
  6. Recipes: Portion-size adjustment was validated as useful by all participants and supports waste reduction.
  7. Meal Planner: A/B testing favored full-week display for flexibility and alignment with weekly shopping habits. Food Item vs Recipe separation also improved logging flexibility.
  8. Shopping: Shopping List and Shopping Cart mental models were intuitive due to familiarity with e-commerce flows.

Mid-Fi Prototype

Design Considerations

  1. Color Scheme: Green-forward palette reinforces sustainability goals while maintaining readability and contrast.
  2. Fresh Food Expiry Tracking: Expiry logic for fresh produce is based on item shelf-life data to improve practical accuracy.
  3. Storage Display: Bottom display was selected for better balance between visibility and simplicity.
  4. Family Integration: Users can join/leave families and add members for better list and pantry coordination.
  5. Receipt Scanning: De-prioritized due to missing expiry data and unclear abbreviations; manual expiry input remains the more reliable path.
  6. Profile Border: Replaced with profile-picture differentiation to reduce confusion and clutter.
  7. Diet Tracker: Moved to Profile as an optional add-on so pantry management remains the core function.
  8. Input Methods: Voice, barcode, and text consolidated into one Google-style input pattern for faster capture.
  9. Recipe Filter: Overlapping filters were merged, with ingredient-availability highlighting for clarity.
  10. Pantry Picks: Renamed for clarity and aligned with user intent around cooking from existing inventory.
  11. Meal Planner Integration: Added quick recipe-to-planner action to support planning while browsing.

Lo-Fi to Mid-Fi Design Considerations

  1. Navigation Bar Activation: Hollow (inactive) and filled (active) icon treatment improved contrast and state visibility.
  2. Family and Diet Tracker Features: Card layouts with icons made both features more discoverable and engaging.
  3. Speech Input Template: Reworded from Use this template to Speak in the following format for clearer instruction.

Limitations of the Current Design

  1. Image Use: Heavy image usage increases app size and may reduce accessibility for lower-spec devices or limited data plans.
  2. Database Size: A universal grocery database remains difficult to maintain comprehensively across item varieties.
Chapter 04 · The decisions

Five calls, with the reasoning showing

Decision · IA

Pantry-first, diet-second (even though diet is the hook)

What I did
Kept the Diet Tracker opt-in and moved it into the Profile tab, not the main nav.
Why
The product bet is that diet tracking brings users in, but the pantry manager is what fights food waste. The IA has to reflect the product's purpose, not its acquisition funnel. If I'd put Diet Tracker front and center, I'd have built exactly the product I was trying to differentiate from.
Decision · UI

Grid view over list view for the pantry

What I did
A/B-tested grid, list, and a toggle between them. All 3 users picked grid. I shipped grid with no toggle.
Why
Users identify groceries by packaging, not by name. A toggle would have hedged the decision and added a setting nobody asked for. If users agree, don't make them configure.
Tradeoff I accepted
Image-dependency inflates app size and bandwidth. I named this explicitly in the limitations section.
Decision · Interaction

One search bar, three input modalities

What I did
Replaced separate Voice / Barcode / Text tabs (my lo-fi version) with a Google-style search bar containing mic and barcode icons inline.
Why
Grocery shopping is one-handed and rushed. Tabs add a "choose method, then input" cognitive step. The unified bar borrows a pattern every Singaporean uses twenty times a day — the decision cost drops to zero.
Decision · Scope

Saying no to receipt scanning, a requested feature

What I did
Didn't build it, despite it being asked for.
Why
Receipts use abbreviated product names and omit expiry dates. A half-working scanner would train users to distrust the data. And the manual expiry-entry step is itself part of the behavior change — it forces the user to ask: will I actually eat this before it expires?
Decision · Process

Heuristic evaluation before more user tests, not after

What I did
Ran a full Nielsen 10-heuristic audit on the mid-fi prototype, scored by severity × frequency × persistence, and fixed all Severity-3 and 4 issues before scheduling the next round of users.
Why
Users' time is the scarcest resource in the loop. Letting someone trip over a Severity-4 issue I could have caught myself wastes their session and skews every insight that follows. Heuristics are the cheap filter; users are the expensive one.

Three Wizard-of-Oz sessions with 18–24-year-olds, each walking through a seven-task script: sign up, activate diet tracker, add granola, handle expiring granola, meal-plan a recipe, shop for missing ingredients, and stock the pantry.

What held up

  • The Diet Tracker + Pantry combo was recognized as a product category all 3 users said they'd download — the Trojan-horse hypothesis survived contact with users.
  • Grid view beat list view 3/0. The reason users gave was the one I hadn't predicted: they recognize groceries by packaging.
  • The Shopping List + Shopping Cart mental model mapped cleanly onto the users' existing mental model of e-commerce, which made it immediately navigable.

What broke

  • Non-customizable pantry sections — Severity 4. Users in the real world have varying numbers of pantry areas and call them different things. Fixed: added add/remove/rename.
  • Inflexible grocery quantities — Severity 4. Users couldn't add a partial or custom quantity. Fixed: dual-input (standard + custom).
  • Editable vs. non-editable fields visually indistinguishable — Severity 3. Fixed with a filled-vs-outlined convention.
  • Diet Tracker onboarding lacked explanation — Severity 3. Fixed with a feature-list tooltip.
Chapter 06 · Reflection

What I'd do differently

★ What I learned

The product strategy carried the design more than I expected. If I did this again, I'd prototype the acquisition funnel first and the pantry features second — the opposite of how I actually worked.

My sample size was 2 interviews and 3 usability sessions. That's enough for directional signal but not for statistical confidence. A follow-up with 8–10 users per round would catch issues my small N missed.

Heuristic evaluation caught two catastrophes that user testing did not. It's now a default step in my process, not an optional one.

What comes next

Plans for Future Enhancements

Several features and improvements are planned for the future but remain beyond current time constraints:

  1. Customizable Recipes: Allow users to modify recipes by adjusting ingredients and estimate calories based on those changes, providing more flexibility.
  2. Gamification: Introduce gamification to track food-waste reduction progress, potentially adding a competitive element for families to engage with.
  3. Ingredient History: Add a history of ingredient usage to help users track and manage their pantry more effectively.
  4. In-App Tutorial: Integrate a tutorial to raise visibility of the Diet Tracker feature and guide new users through the app.
  5. Add Grocery Functionality: Enable users to add grocery items manually, filling in gaps left by the database.
  6. Customizable Expiry Date Tracker: Allow users to adjust when expiry date alerts and color changes occur on grocery cards, offering greater flexibility.
  7. Help Center: Implement a help and support section for users who need assistance, addressing the need for documentation and user guidance.
  8. Exercise Tracker: Expand the Diet Tracker to include an exercise tracker, linking diet and activity, but requires testing for potential complexity overload.
  9. Educational Tips: Introduce educational content to inform users about food waste reduction and align more closely with PantryPal's mission.

Key Testing and Mobile Context Considerations

The most critical test that remains is ensuring the app works well in mobile contexts, given that grocery shopping frequently takes place in dynamic, on-the-go environments. This needs to be a key focus moving forward.
