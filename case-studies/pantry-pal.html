Chapter 01 · Context

A waste problem the city already knew about

Singapore's Semakau landfill is projected to run out of space by 2035. The government has a Zero Waste Masterplan, the NEA runs annual reduction campaigns, and NGOs like ZeroWasteSG have been educating consumers for years. And yet food waste in 2023 — 755,000 tonnes — was higher than in 2020.

The education-first approach had hit a ceiling. My interview subjects, both 23-year-old Singaporeans, confirmed it in the bluntest possible way: they knew exactly why food waste mattered, and they still didn't change their behavior. The gap between knowledge and action was the real problem — and no existing app was addressing it for the demographic that needed it most.

I knew about it, I just didn't care enough to do anything differently.

That quote — paraphrased from interview #1 — reframed the brief. The question wasn't "how do we teach people about food waste?" It was "how do we get people who already know to act?"

Chapter 02 · The strategy

A Trojan horse for behavior change

The competitive landscape had a gap and a cautionary tale, both relevant.

The gap: Treatsure and Olio addressed waste after purchase — connecting consumers to surplus food that already existed. But most household waste is generated upstream of that, when people buy more than they need in the first place.

The cautionary tale: KitchenPal was the only pre-purchase pantry manager in the market. It hadn't scaled. The reason, I suspected, was that pantry management is a chore nobody opens an app for. If the motivation gap was the real problem, building a better pantry app was solving the wrong thing.

So I stopped designing a pantry app.

I designed a diet tracker that happens to have a pantry in it. Diet tracking — by way of apps like MyFitnessPal — is already a daily habit for a significant slice of 18–24-year-olds. Logging a meal means logging ingredients. Logging ingredients means knowing what's in your pantry. Knowing what's in your pantry means not over-buying. The waste-reduction behaviors ride the rails of a motivation that already exists.

This is the single most important decision in the project. Everything else follows from it.

Five features, each earning its place

FeatureWhy it exists

Chapter 03 · The decisions

Five calls, with the reasoning showing

Decision · IA

Pantry-first, diet-second (even though diet is the hook)

What I did
Kept the Diet Tracker opt-in and moved it into the Profile tab, not the main nav.
Why
The product bet is that diet tracking brings users in, but the pantry manager is what fights food waste. The IA has to reflect the product's purpose, not its acquisition funnel. If I'd put Diet Tracker front and center, I'd have built exactly the product I was trying to differentiate from.
Decision · UI

Grid view over list view for the pantry

What I did
A/B-tested grid, list, and a toggle between them. All 3 users picked grid. I shipped grid with no toggle.
Why
Users identify groceries by packaging, not by name. A toggle would have hedged the decision and added a setting nobody asked for. If users agree, don't make them configure.
Tradeoff I accepted
Image-dependency inflates app size and bandwidth. I named this explicitly in the limitations section.
Decision · Interaction

One search bar, three input modalities

What I did
Replaced separate Voice / Barcode / Text tabs (my lo-fi version) with a Google-style search bar containing mic and barcode icons inline.
Why
Grocery shopping is one-handed and rushed. Tabs add a "choose method, then input" cognitive step. The unified bar borrows a pattern every Singaporean uses twenty times a day — the decision cost drops to zero.
Decision · Scope

Saying no to receipt scanning, a requested feature

What I did
Didn't build it, despite it being asked for.
Why
Receipts use abbreviated product names and omit expiry dates. A half-working scanner would train users to distrust the data. And the manual expiry-entry step is itself part of the behavior change — it forces the user to ask: will I actually eat this before it expires?
Decision · Process

Heuristic evaluation before more user tests, not after

What I did
Ran a full Nielsen 10-heuristic audit on the mid-fi prototype, scored by severity × frequency × persistence, and fixed all Severity-3 and 4 issues before scheduling the next round of users.
Why
Users' time is the scarcest resource in the loop. Letting someone trip over a Severity-4 issue I could have caught myself wastes their session and skews every insight that follows. Heuristics are the cheap filter; users are the expensive one.
Chapter 04 · Validation

What the testing actually proved

Three Wizard-of-Oz sessions with 18–24-year-olds, each walking through a seven-task script: sign up, activate diet tracker, add granola, handle expiring granola, meal-plan a recipe, shop for missing ingredients, and stock the pantry.

What held up

  • The Diet Tracker + Pantry combo was recognized as a product category all 3 users said they'd download — the Trojan-horse hypothesis survived contact with users.
  • Grid view beat list view 3/0. The reason users gave was the one I hadn't predicted: they recognize groceries by packaging.
  • The Shopping List + Shopping Cart mental model mapped cleanly onto the users' existing mental model of e-commerce, which made it immediately navigable.

What broke

  • Non-customizable pantry sections — Severity 4. Users in the real world have varying numbers of pantry areas and call them different things. Fixed: added add/remove/rename.
  • Inflexible grocery quantities — Severity 4. Users couldn't add a partial or custom quantity. Fixed: dual-input (standard + custom).
  • Editable vs. non-editable fields visually indistinguishable — Severity 3. Fixed with a filled-vs-outlined convention.
  • Diet Tracker onboarding lacked explanation — Severity 3. Fixed with a feature-list tooltip.
Chapter 05 · Reflection

What I'd do differently

★ What I learned

The product strategy carried the design more than I expected. If I did this again, I'd prototype the acquisition funnel first and the pantry features second — the opposite of how I actually worked.

My sample size was 2 interviews and 3 usability sessions. That's enough for directional signal but not for statistical confidence. A follow-up with 8–10 users per round would catch issues my small N missed.

Heuristic evaluation caught two catastrophes that user testing did not. It's now a default step in my process, not an optional one.

What comes next

  1. Next: Gamification, tutorial, and help center — fix the Diet Tracker discoverability issue I couldn't solve in this pass.
  2. Later: Ingredient history, exercise tracker, education content.
  3. Maybe, deliberately parked: Receipt scanning. Revisit only when OCR + product databases are reliable enough to not train distrust.

The single biggest untested variable: the mobile context. I designed for it, but I haven't yet tested the prototype in an actual grocery store, one-handed, in a hurry. That's the first thing I'd do in v2.
