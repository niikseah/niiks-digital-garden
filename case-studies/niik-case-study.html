01Theme
Magazine
Instead of the usual brand style guide, I decided to make mine more interesting by following the concept of a magazine to flip through.
02Logo
Hybrid wordmark + interlocking monogram
My logo plays on the two i's in my name. I deliberately emphasized this and aligned it with the saying: "An eye for design". Hence, the eye test. My name itself—Niik—offered an aesthetic and unique structure that could form the foundation of my logo. I decided to highlight the two “I’s” in my name to symbolize “eyes” for creativity. This led me to a design where the letters “N” and “K” merged together, with the two dots above and below the name forming a symmetrical, eye-catching motif. The use of the color Flame Pea for the dots ties directly to the theme of creativity. I created scalable versions of my logo, ensuring that it could adapt to different sizes and contexts. The final version of the logo retains its iconicity even when scaled down, making it versatile.
03Color
Meaningful color traits
Each colour represents a specific trait I embody.
Flame Pea represents my creativeness,
Observatory represents my desire to grow,
Outer Space represents my quiet focus,
Spring Wood represents my love for clean designs.

• Flame Pea (Orange-Red): This color symbolizes creativity and passion for design, making it the dominant accent color in my brand. It's a vibrant hue that draws attention without being overpowering.

• Observatory (Green): This color conveys growth, an essential value in both my personal and professional life. It serves as a complementary tone to Flame Pea.

• Outer Space (Grey-Black): Representing composure, and maturity, this color grounds the brand in elegance and discipline.

• Spring Wood (Muted Yellow-White): A soft, neutral tone that contrasts beautifully with the brighter hues, Spring Wood adds a clean and fresh feeling to the overall palette.

The combination of Flame Pea with Observatory and Outer Space creates a complementary and harmonious feel, while Spring Wood serves as a neutral backdrop, allowing the stronger colors to stand out.
04Typography
Neue Haas Grotesk Display + Qanelas
Neue Haas for editorial precision; Qanelas for bold display weight. Pairing balances information density (resume) with impact (magazine cover) inside one type system. For typography, I sought fonts that embodied a clean and minimalistic style while also standing out. I chose Neue Haas Grotesk Display Pro for its sleek, modern appearance, with its clean lines perfectly matching my desire for simplicity, and its professional look aligns with my design ethos. To balance the cleanliness of the primary font, I chose Qanelas-a bold, modern typeface that brings weight to headers and key text elements. The font's elegant yet robust structure complements the streamlined nature of the rest of the design.
05Name card
Reframed as a corporate access card with QR
Traditional name cards feel obsolete. Recasting it as a lanyard ID solved a real interaction problem (memorability + digital handoff) and turned a static deliverable into a wearable artifact. While the concept of traditional name cards felt outdated to me, I still wanted to retain a form of physical representation that aligns with my brand. Inspired by modern access cards, I incorporated a QR code into the design. This allowed me to provide additional information through a digital format, linking to my portfolio or contact details.
01 — REFRAMING THE BRIEF

The most interesting decision was not designing a name card at all.

The deliverable list called for a name card. My honest reaction: I haven't received one in years, and I rarely keep the ones I'm given. Designing a beautiful name card felt like polishing an interface nobody opens.

So I reframed the problem. What's the job a name card is hired to do? Three things: identify me, make me memorable, hand off contact info. A traditional rectangle is one possible solution. So is a corporate access card on a lanyard.

The Why — Solving the Job, Not the Brief

I drew inspiration from a runway show that played on corporate-wear access cards, plus the iconic Anne Hathaway intern look in The Devil Wears Prada. By recasting the name card as a wearable lanyard ID with a QR code, I solved the real problem (memorable handoff) better than the literal brief would have. Strong work answers the question behind the question.

The QR replaces a wall of contact text with a scan, which keeps the front of the card minimal — logo, name, role — and pushes the data layer to the back. Front-of-card is identity. Back-of-card is utility. Same separation of concerns as a well-architected page.

02 — CRITIQUE AS A FILTER

Knowing how to be discerning about the feedback I incorporate.

Two rounds of critique shaped the final output.

Took it — alignment bug. Anna noticed the N on my magazine cover wasn't consistently left-aligned across spreads. I hadn't caught it. Fixed immediately. This was a craft issue, full stop.

Took it — contrast failure. The combination of Outer Space type on Flame Pea blocks tanked legibility in resume body copy. I swapped resume body to Spring Wood. This was an accessibility-adjacent issue masquerading as a stylistic one.

Set it aside — pattern overlay on resume. One suggestion was to add the brand pattern to the resume itself. I agreed it would have made the resume more visually interesting, but I disagreed about whether that served the user. Resumes are scanned in 6 seconds by recruiters. Pattern would have added noise to a surface where speed-of-read is the only metric that matters.

"Junior designers take all feedback. Senior designers take feedback that serves the user. Knowing which is which is the actual job."
