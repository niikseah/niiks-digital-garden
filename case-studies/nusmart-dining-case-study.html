Problem

The queue was lying.

Users picked stalls based on “no. of orders waiting” — a number that excluded walk-ins and ignored stall speed. Result: anxiety, late returns to class, app abandonment.

Insight

Trust beats precision.

Students didn't need a perfect ETA. They needed a reliable one — and a way to feel their order moving without walking to the stall to verify it.

Solution

From a number to a signal.

Replaced ambiguous order counts with a colour-coded wait-time estimate, a live position-in-queue tracker, and a glanceable lock-screen notification.

Loop

The system learns.

Post-collection feedback prompt feeds actual wait times back into the estimate model — closing the data loop that the original app never had.

01 · Situation

A campus app no one wanted to use.

NUSmart Dining is a mobile app launched in 2020 to relieve canteen queues for ~40,000 students and staff at National University of Singapore. The app enables users to pre-order and pre-pay for meals, saving the trouble of queueing in person. This saves both stallowners' and users' time, increasing convenience, facilitating food ordering to alleviate crowds.

Students were ordering ahead, walking to the canteen, and discovering their food hadn't been started — or worse, that the “3 orders waiting” signal they trusted was off by an order of magnitude because it didn't count walk-ins.

02 · Action — Research

Triangulating, not just collecting.

I designed a three-method research protocol — chosen specifically so each method's weakness was covered by another's strength:

Contextual Inquiry — 2 students, in canteen

Behaviour

Master-apprentice protocol across the full ordering flow. Captured what people did, not what they remembered doing. This is where we caught the “walking to verify” behaviour that surveys completely missed.

Semi-structured interviews — 2 students

Emotion

“Describe the app in three words” surfaced the affective layer — words like frustrating, unreliable, anxious. These wouldn't come up in observation.

Survey — 23 students

Validation

Open to non-users too, so we could understand why people had churned. This validated that our 4 deep-research participants weren't outliers.

Why this mix?

A 23-person survey alone would have given us breadth without depth — we'd have known that people were frustrated, not why. CIs alone (n=2) wouldn't survive a recruiter's “how do you know that's representative?” question. The survey was the validation layer.

We synthesised everything in Figma using affinity mapping and an empathy map, which surfaced three candidate pain points.

03 · Action — The hard call

Picking one problem to solve.

Several pain points in using NUSmart Dining have been identified, including unclear order status updates and confusing error messages. Users express frustration with the difficulty of communicating with stall owners and the lack of feedback on order statuses. Additionally, stalls serving food like economical rice offer limited customisation options, which complicates the ordering process.

Three pain points emerged. Junior teams try to fix all three. I made the case for cutting two:

① Order status updates from stallholders

Cut — out of scope

High user value, but the fix lives on the stallholder side of the app — a stakeholder we couldn't access in 3 weeks. Designing in a vacuum here would have been theatre.

② Wait-time clarity across stalls

Picked

High value, addressable on the user side, and testable with a paper prototype. Sweet spot.

③ Customisation flexibility

Cut — narrow value

Real frustration, but only for stalls that require customisation (caifan, mala). Solving it wouldn't move the needle on the central trust problem.

The PM lesson here

A scope cut you can defend is more valuable than a scope you can't ship. I used a simple value-vs-feasibility argument to align the team — the same kind of trade-off framing I'd use in a roadmap review.

04 · Action — Design decisions

Three decisions, one philosophy.

The throughline: turn an ambiguous number into a trustworthy signal.

Killed “no. of orders waiting”

Information design

Users couldn't tell what the number meant — did it include walk-ins? Did it weight stall speed? We replaced it with a direct minute estimate.

Traffic-light colour coding (green <5 min, yellow 5-9, red 10+)

Design system

Chose semantic colours users already know from traffic lights, crowd-level indicators, and Apple Maps. Zero learning curve, glanceable in 1 second — critical when a student is choosing a stall between classes.

Live position-in-queue + lock-screen notification

Mental model

Mirrored the physical queue mental model — orders ahead of you visibly leave the screen as they're prepared. The lock-screen widget means students can stop opening the app every 90 seconds.

Post-collection feedback loop

System design

One question after pickup: “How long did you actually wait?” This crowdsources ground truth, so the estimate model improves over time without needing stallholder input — addressing the original constraint.

Before

“3 orders waiting”

Decontextualised, excluded walk-ins, ignored stall speed, no progress feedback after order.

After

“~12 min · you're 4th”

Direct minute estimate · live queue position · lock-screen tracking · feedback loop closes itself.

05 · Result

What we shipped — and how I'd measure it.

A redesigned NUSmart Dining to improve communication between users and stall owners for orderly and timely updates on the queue situation at canteens and their orders for a seamless meal experience.

The deliverable was a hi-fi prototype validated through paper prototyping and a redesigned user journey. Because this was a course-scoped project, I can't claim shipped-app metrics — but I can be specific about what success would look like and how I'd instrument it.

Why these metrics

App Store rating is the proxy users themselves chose to score the app — fixing it is the public-facing success signal. The “walked to verify” metric maps directly to the behaviour we observed in CIs. Estimate accuracy is the leading indicator that tells us whether the feedback loop is actually working before ratings move.

Design Process

Empathise: User Research

We conducted three types of user research: Contextual Inquiries (CIs) on 2 individuals, Interviews on 2 individuals, and Surveys on 23 individuals, to discover more about our primary users' (NUS students) journey in using NUSmart Dining - to understand their experience and identify any pain points.

Pain Points

  1. NUS students need accurate and timely order updates and communication from stall owners because unclear and unreliable order tracking forces them to physically check on their orders.
  2. NUS students need a clear understanding of wait time across stalls to make informed choices about where to order.
  3. NUS students need greater flexibility in customising their food because the current options are limited, which makes it hard for them to order items to their liking.

The scope of the project required the group to work on 1 pain point, and we chose to focus on creating a clearer understanding of wait times across stalls (Problem 2).

Define: Processing of User Research

Following our research process, we visualised the data collected using empathy maps and organised them into common themes via affinity mapping.

Overarching Issues

  1. Uncertainty regarding waiting time at stalls and one's order due to factors like a lack of transparency and understanding about order progress.
  2. Inconsistency and unreliability in information that depended heavily on stallholders' updates.
  3. Concerns about availability — users reported that some stalls were stated as “closed” and unavailable for ordering on the app despite being open for orders in person.
  4. Difficulties encountered when attempting to customise their orders.

With the problems identified, we identified a persona, Zach, and his user journey, to allow us to better visualise the user profile we were designing for.

Current Scenario

Zach takes his phone out 10 minutes before class ends, and opens the NUSmart Dining Application before peak lunch hours. His next class is in less than an hour, and he needs to get his lunch before it starts. He looks through the list of canteens and picks The Deck, which the app states is the canteen closest to his classroom.

He looks through the list of stalls at The Deck on the application and looks at the “no. of orders waiting” at each stall, hoping to look for one that can get him his food the fastest. Seeing that the Japanese stall has only three orders waiting, he orders from the stall. However, once he reached the Deck canteen, he noticed a spiralling queue of about 10 people in front of the Japanese store he ordered from.

At first, he felt a sense of relief since he already placed his order on the application so he sat down at an empty table and proceeded to wait while periodically checking his order status on the application. However, 15 minutes passed, and he realised that his order status had still remained as “processing” and the people he saw previously in the queue had started to collect their food.

So he headed down to the stall to check on the status of his order. This is when he realised that the stall had not started on his order, wanting to prioritise those who queued up physically. Zach gets agitated and seeing this, the aunty tells him that she will make his order now. He collects his order and rushes to finish his food. He could not enjoy his meal since he was rushing. He then proceeded to his class but was about 5 minutes late. The next day when he wants to get his lunch again, he is reminded of his previous experience and decides to skip his lunch instead.

Proposed Scenario

Zach takes his phone out 10 minutes before class ends and opens the NUSmart Dining Application before peak lunch hours. His next class is in less than an hour, and he needs to get his lunch before it starts. He looks through canteens and picks The Deck.

He looks through the list of stalls and uses estimated waiting time with colour coding to scan quickly. Seeing that the Japanese stall has around 15 minutes of waiting time, he orders from the stall hoping he can collect food when class ends. Once ordered, the app displays orders before him and estimated completion time.

After class, he notices a queue in person but does not feel anxious because the app clearly states that his order is next to be prepared. He talks to a friend and occasionally glances at lock-screen notifications that show live progress. Once ready, the phone vibrates and he collects his order.

After collecting his food, the app asks how long he waited. He answers, finishes his meal peacefully, and still has time before class.

With the persona and user journey fleshed out, we identified “How Might We?” opportunities:

  • How might we allow users to make better-informed decisions on which stall to order from?
  • How might we make waiting times on the application more accurate?
  • How might we make order tracking more seamless across classroom, walking, and canteen?

Cost of Work

We estimated project cost based on experience level, complexity, scope, device target, and timeline urgency.

As entry-level designers, we scoped for a lower rate. Complexity was moderate-low (focused redesign of one pain point). Scope included affinity diagramming, empathy maps (x4), scenarios (x2), user journey maps (x2), navigation flows (x2), interviews (x2), surveys (~20), CIs (x2), wireframes, Crazy 8s, and paper prototypes.

We invested about 10 hours/week for 3 weeks (30 hours total). Using an entry-level benchmark of $20–$40/hour, the estimated cost range is approximately $600 to $1200, primarily labour for research, synthesis, and ideation.

If progressed further, additional costs would include feature expansion, user testing rounds, and iterative revisions, likely billed hourly.

06 · Reflection

What I'd do differently.

Talk to stallholders earlier ✦

We scoped them out for time reasons — but even one 30-min interview would have stress-tested whether our crowdsourced-feedback model is actually realistic, or whether stallholders would resent yet another data input.

Run a usability test on the hi-fi

We tested the paper prototype but not the final wireframes. With one more week, I'd have run a 5-user test on the live-queue interaction specifically — that's the most novel pattern, and the most likely to confuse.

Accessibility audit on the colour system

Traffic-light semantics carry real risk for the ~8% of male users with red-green colour deficiency. The fix is small (add an icon or shape modifier alongside colour) — but it should have been in v1, not v2.

Pressure-test the estimate model

“Crowdsource accurate wait times from users” sounds clean on a slide. In practice, response rates drop fast — I'd want to model what happens to the estimate when only ~15% of users complete the feedback prompt.

07 · Takeaway

The transferable principle.

When a product loses user trust, the instinct is to add features. The harder, better move is to find the one signal users have already stopped believing — and rebuild that signal end-to-end. ✦

For NUSmart Dining, that signal was the queue. Everything else — the customisation gripes, the UI complaints — flowed from the fact that users couldn't trust the app to tell them the truth about waiting. Fix the truth-telling, and the rest gets cheaper to fix.
